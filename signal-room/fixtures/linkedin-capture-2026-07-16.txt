A recent Korean livestreamer broke down on camera after losing everything in a single trade, a painful and public reminder of what happens when greed and FOMO takes over. 

I have been spending time recently with Howard Marks' memos and Mohnish Pabrai's podcasts, and it sparked a question: how can investors win in an era where information is instant and AI can do the analysis in seconds?

Think about what is available to anyone with an internet connection today. Real-time financial reports. Breaking news. Probabilistic signals from insiders via prediction markets like Polymarkets. And now, AI that can research, analyse, and model an entire business and its financials faster than most of us can make a cup of coffee.

The traditional edges are eroding fast. Superior financial modelling skills? Democratised. Asymmetric access to information? Largely gone. The playing field has never been more level, which paradoxically makes it harder, not easier, to outperform.

So where does the edge come from now? I believe what matters most is Temperament.

Because here is what never changes, regardless of how much technology advances. Human nature. We are wired to be greedy, fearful, impatient, and tribal. No software update fixes that.

As Howard Marks put it, the biggest investing errors come not from informational or analytical failures, but from psychological ones.

Look around and the evidence is everywhere. South Korean retail investors piling into overleveraged, momentum-driven trades trying to chase unrealistic returns. The countless investors who rode crypto to extraordinary paper gains and then watched it all evaporate, unable to walk away. And perhaps most painfully, the investors who made a genuinely sound call, only to panic and sell the moment the market moved against them, giving up the very return they had done the hard work to earn.

The market does not reward the smartest person in the room. It rewards the most disciplined one.

Could AI help here, acting as a kind of rational guardrail against our worst psychological impulses? Possibly. But as long as humans retain the ability to override decisions, and we always will, the emotional pull of a falling portfolio will remain a very human problem to solve.

The ultimate edge any investor can build is not a better spreadsheet or a faster data feed. It is control over themselves.

We're auditing AI's hallucination rate in legal domains but nobody's auditing the bar.

India's Supreme Court released draft AI regulations for courts last month.
AI is now permitted for legal research, contract drafting, citation checks, translation, and case management.
The condition is clear: AI assists. Attorneys decide. Accountability stays human.
That's the right architecture.

We debate AI's hallucination rate — a real risk, worth taking seriously.
Meanwhile, the Bar Council of India Chairman recently stated that 35–40% of practising advocates may hold fake or unverifiable degrees.
When BCI launched a verification drive, 40% of lawyers didn't submit their credentials at all.

AI hallucinates citations. Some lawyers have apparently hallucinated their entire qualification.

Both are accountability gaps. Only one gets the headlines.

I have had the opportunity to interact with multiple lawyers about where they actually want AI.
Two use cases came up every time:

— Long contracts: identifying every enforceable clause in a 200-page agreement. Not a summary. The clause buried on page 147 that changes everything. AI does this tirelessly, without billing by the hour.

— Contract oracles: when a dispute arises about whether a condition has been met, AI surfaces what the clause actually says in context — like resolution arbiters in prediction markets. Not the judge. The reference. Human signs off. Always.

AI in law will still hallucinate. The answer isn't avoidance.
It's what the SC draft already prescribes: Disclose. Verify. Own the outcome.

One question worth sitting with:
Would you rather be represented by an AI working under a qualified, accountable attorney - or a human in a black coat whose degree no one has verified?

As businesses continue to explore AI's potential, it's crucial to consider how best to integrate these technologies in ways that enhance, rather than replace, the human elements of management. 예측시장(Prediction Market)은 특정 미래 사건의 발생 여부를 두고 주식처럼 계약을 사고파는 혁신적인 금융 및 정보 플랫폼입니다. 미국 대선 등 글로벌 이벤트를 기점으로 시장 규모가 급성장했으며, 단순한 베팅을 넘어 집단지성을 활용한 미래 데이터 예측 도구로 주목받고 있습니다.요청하신 기사 목차와 의문점들을 바탕으로 예측시장의 핵심 메커니즘, 한국 내 규제 현황, 그리고 투자자 주의 사항을 명확히 정리해 드립니다.

1. 메타도 뛰어든 ‘내일의 시장’ (작동 원리)
계약의 구조: "A 후보가 당선될 것인가?" 같은 질문에 'Yes' 또는 'No' 토큰을 구매합니다.
가격의 결정: 사건이 실현되면 최종 가치는 1달러(또는 100% 가치)가 되고, 실패하면 0달러가 됩니다.
확률의 시각화: 특정 토큰이 0.6달러에 거래된다면 시장은 그 사건의 발생 확률을 60%로 보고 있다는 뜻입니다.글로벌 트렌드: 폴리마켓(Polymarket) 같은 탈중앙화 플랫폼이 시장을 주도하고 있으며, 빅테크 기업들도 사내 의사결정 및 데이터 수집을 위해 예측시장 메커니즘을 적극 도입하고 있습니다.

2. 베팅보다 비싼 데이터 & 집단지성의 힘 (3~4번 내용)돈의 무게: 단순히 설문조사에 답할 때와 달리, 자신의 돈을 걸면 사람들은 훨씬 더 객관적이고 필사적으로 정보를 분석합니다.

확률을 망치는 리스크: 거대 자본을 가진 세력이 승률을 조작하기 위해 고의로 대량 매수를 하거나(여론 조작), 편향된 군중 심리로 인해 일시적으로 확률이 왜곡되는 '도박판'의 한계도 분명히 존재합니다.

3. 규제에 막힌 한국형 폴리마켓 (합법 유무)
한국 내 서비스 부재 이유: 대한민국 법상 정부가 허가한 자(토토, 경륜, 카지노 등) 외의 사행성 베팅은 자본시장법 및 사행행위규제법 위반입니다. 민간 기업이 한국에서 폴리마켓 같은 서비스를 합법적으로 출시하는 것은 현재 불가능합니다.

Live update on my prediction-market microstructure research: the regime filter is earning its keep.

A few weeks ago I shared research on regime-conditioned mispricing in sub-hourly crypto prediction markets. Since then, the system has been trading live — small size, deliberately — and this week produced the result I care most about.

Not the P&L curve (though today was green, screenshot below). The shape change.

The story in one line: same strategy, one added condition — and the equity curve flipped from bleeding to climbing.

The condition came from an observation that's obvious in hindsight: in these markets, chop predicts chop. A contract whose implied leader has already flip-flopped in the final pre-settlement window keeps flip-flopping; one with a stable leader tends to stay put. Filtering on stability — measured from the market being traded, not the underlying — was the only reversal filter (of 13 candidates tested) that survived a train/hold-out split. Every clever alternative — analytic win-probability models, volatility regimes on the underlying, order-book imbalance, cross-asset chop — looked great in-sample and died out-of-sample.

Live lessons worth passing on:
1️⃣ The market you trade knows more than the asset it references. Every signal derived from the underlying's price action lost to signals derived from the contract's own order flow. The crowd's pricing already contains the candles.
2️⃣ Live edge ≠ backtest edge. My hold-out said one number; live trading says a smaller one. The gap itself is data — it measures how crowded the trade has become since the sample period.
3️⃣ Discipline is the strategy. Pre-registered checkpoints, a hard capital floor, one hypothesis promoted at a time, everything else in shadow-logging until it proves itself on live fills. The graveyard of my rejected ideas is now 13 deep, and it's the most valuable dataset I own.
4️⃣ Distance to the settlement line matters. I’ve also been shadow-logging a Window Delta filter: measuring the distance between the current BTC price and the opening price of the resolution window (sourced from Binance). The hypothesis is simple — when price is too close to the line, the market becomes more vulnerable to late reversals before settlement. Rather than forcing another rule into production, it stays in observation mode until live data proves its value.

Methodology open, parameters withheld, as always. Not financial advice — positions can lose the full stake, and mine sometimes do. That's in the data too.
