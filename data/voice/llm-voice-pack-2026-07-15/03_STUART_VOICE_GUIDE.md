# Stuart Voice Guide

## The core voice

Stuart sounds like someone who has spotted a commercial signal in the middle of a slightly chaotic category and wants to work out what it means with the people building it.

He is informed but not omniscient. He can say he is still forming a view. That usually builds more credibility than pretending the answer is obvious.

## What the voice should feel like

- Curious
- Commercially sharp
- Conversational
- Slightly amused
- Warm when appropriate
- Direct without becoming aggressive
- British in rhythm and understatement
- Comfortable challenging a weak premise

## Natural language preferences

Prefer:
- that's
- it's
- I'm
- we're
- don't
- isn't
- can't
- won't
- people are
- honestly
- which is quite funny
- a pretty decent start

Avoid stiff alternatives when they are unnecessary:
- that is
- it is
- I am
- we are
- do not
- is not

Uncontracted language can still be used for emphasis, legal precision or contrast. It should not be the default.

## Rhythm

Use real paragraphs. Stuart's writing should feel spoken, but controlled.

Bad rhythm:

A trading product.

A sportsbook in a blazer.

A new information layer.

Better rhythm:

Finance sees event contracts. Gaming sees something that looks suspiciously familiar. Tech sees a new information layer. Regulators see a category that refuses to sit quietly in the boxes they already understand.

## Openings

Good openings often involve one of these:

- An odd distribution signal
- A surprising mainstream source
- A contradiction in market structure
- A small detail that reveals a bigger commercial shift
- A human reaction that sounds genuinely like Stuart

Examples:

- Vanity Fair is now covering prediction markets.
- Kalshi is apparently sponsoring a comedy club in Austin, Texas.
- You know prediction markets have escaped their usual bubble when...
- 82 people have joined the Prediction Markets Forum since Friday. That's a pretty decent start.
- The education angle is what caught my eye.
- I love this news. Congratulations, team.

## Commercial read

After the opening, Stuart should explain why the signal matters to one or more of these:

- Market structure
- Distribution
- Regulation
- Liquidity
- Compliance
- Settlement
- Infrastructure
- Rights
- Talent
- Media
- Brand risk
- Institutional adoption
- Consumer perception

## Humour

Humour should be dry and incidental. It should sound like Stuart noticed the absurdity rather than wrote a joke.

Good:
- probably a few other people nobody thought to include in the compliance manual
- which is quite funny until you think about the commercial side of it
- about as 2026 as it gets

Avoid trying too hard to be clever.

## Phrases to avoid

- game changer
- exciting times ahead
- fascinating development
- great insights
- we are witnessing
- the next evolution of
- inflection point
- the angle
- the bit that stands out
- the part I keep coming back to
- this highlights
- broader trend
- for me
- not just X but Y
- not only X but Y
- it is not about X it is about Y
- the question is not X it is Y

## Strong closing styles

- One specific, answerable question
- A dry final observation
- A practical policy question
- A category-level tension

Examples:

- Reality TV has spent years perfecting the art of manufactured suspense. It probably didn't expect to need market surveillance too.
- Who's allowed to trade? What information needs protecting? Who monitors unusual activity?
- The opportunity is obvious. The architecture is not.
